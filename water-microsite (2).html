I

I've never considered myself someone who loves water, yet my life depends on it in more ways than I would usually think about. It doesn't define my identity the way it might for someone who surfs, swims, or spends most weekends on a boat, but I can't imagine my life without it. Asking me to is like asking what life would look like without air, without the sun, without food. It's essential. And yet, every single day I drink it, shower with it, brush my teeth, wash my clothes, and do the dishes with it. So maybe I do love water, but I just never noticed.

II

Reading the Theory of Water made something click for me. I've read this statistic before, but it's different in the way that Leanne Betasamosake Simpson says. She explains that the water on Earth right now is all the water we will ever have. Every drop we use has been cycling through the planet since the beginning. The way the author framed this idea allowed me to understand that water isn't this infinite resource that we can continue to use and waste. Water is a finite system that we are merely borrowing from, and this comes with responsibilities.

III

That idea is the basis of my own theory of water. Water is one of the most necessary things in existence, and one of the most overlooked. It's real, it's valuable, and it shows up in nearly every aspect of daily life, but most of us never take the time to understand the importance of it. The consequences of wasting it aren't immediate, so it's easy to feel like they're not our problem. In the western world especially, water gets treated like an abundance: something to use without thinking. If I held my own daily water usage up to someone living without reliable access to clean water, I'd be embarrassed. But I think small choices matter. If we can build more mindful habits around water usage, those habits add up to something bigger than any one person.

IV

Water doesn't ask anything from us, but it needs our attention. It does what it has always done since the beginning. Because it is always there for us, it is easy to take for granted. My goal with this microsite isn't to lecture anyone or say that I figured everything out. I'm just like most people: someone who uses water every day without thinking twice about it. What I want is to encourage others to look more into what that actually means. How much we use, how much we waste, and what's at risk when we don't pay attention. I think that just looking at it is the first step toward doing something about it, and maybe I can provide that first step to others through this microsite.
